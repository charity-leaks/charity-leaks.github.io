Fishing for rich vilains

So how can we now find out if an entity is an actual charity, and not some other shell corporation with a stolen name?

Checking the directory...

After investigating several methods, we finally chose to check the addresses of the matches we had. By definition, a shell company is a corporation that exists only on paper and has no office and no employees, but may have a bank account, and also needs a mailbox address. It could therefore be assumed that if a real charity’s headquarters match the mail address of a shell with the same name, these are indeed one and the same company.

In the database provided by ICIJ, the majority of the offshore entities have an address registered as a specific node (remember the database organization above). We decided to inspect these addresses for our matches and to compare them with the headquarters addresses of the corresponding charities that were found during the web scraping. If the addresses match, there is a high probability that the offshore accounts do indeed belong to the real charity.

After extracting every nodes with a registered address corresponding to a match we obtained a list of 27 offshore entities. Among them, we discarded every match whose charity headquarter location was not filed in our data. We also removed the entities with an address registered in a Tax Heaven (Barbados, Aruba, Caraïb Islands) when their corresponding charities were located in a different country.

We obtained 11 suspected matches (see below), a dataset small enough for manual inspection. Reading though, we kept only the entities where the countries off the two addresses matched. Some matches are more relevant than other since the town corresponds whereas for other we only have the country.

CAPTURE

In this final list, we found the names of famous charities such as Amnesty International, The Nature Conservancy, World Vision and The American Cancer Society. It is interesting to note that some charities come back two times in our list (The Nature Conservancy and Memorial Cancer Center) with different addresses. Each of these companies is therefore a suspect to us. Before searching the web for potential scandals, we proceeded to the second part of our analysis.

We searched the web for these companies associated with their specific leak source. It seems that the common good is not the main objective of every charity… Indeed, we found that The Nature Conservancy, one of the biggest environmental group was not using the donations for his environmental environment but for investing in oil drilling… : Environmental Groups in-vest in Oil Driling

Same for The Duke Endowment where we found that Duke university uses offshore founds to grow their endowments investing in fossil fuels : US universities use offshore funds to grow their huge endowmnens

These results show that our first investigation method was relevant : Sherlock would have been proud. We did not find any signs of fraud for the other member of our list but a lot of article on the appearance of Amnesty International in the leaks. In these articles, Amnesty International denied being implicated and denounced the use of his name. Despite these article or the lack of data on the implication of these charities, we choose to not put aside these suspect and to keep it in custody while proceeding to the second part of our analysis.

In good or bad company ?

We chose to evaluate the first-level connections of our matches in order to detect clusters. Indeed, intermediaries are crucial for an offshore service provider, handling the administration of the shell companies. If several suspected charities are all connected by a single intermediary, it could mean that said intermediary is specialized in charities. But if one of the connected entities is clearly a fake, there is a high probability than its neighbours are fake too. In this case, it would mean that the given intermediary is in the habit of stealing charity names for its shell companies.

To get these graphs, we extracted every node corresponding to a matched entity and its associated edges. The actors at the other end of these edges were extracted as well. With these data we could represent the first level connections for every entity in a form of a network and see the degree of interconnectivity between the matches.

GRAPH PANAMA

This graph is already very interesting to look at. About half of all the charity nodes (in yellow) are somehow connected to another charity node, which is a lot for a dataset containing over 200'000 nodes. This confirms our suspicions from above that there must be intermediaries who either specialize in charities , or are in the habit of using their names. A closer look shows us some familiar names, such as the World Wildlife Fund and UNICEF, con-nected through the Tarbes Trust.

GRAPH PARADISE1

This graph is much too large for us to handle, so we filter it down, removing any non-charity nodes that are only connected to one other node (an therefore aren't connecting anyone).

GRAPH PARADISE2

We now have a much clearer image of the connecting nodes, and we notice several things. First, UNICEF and the World Wildlife Fund are back, but this time they are connected to the "Internation Red Cross", which the real Red Cross insists is a fake : Misuse of ICRC identity. This is a strong argument for UNICEF and the World Wildlife Fund also being stolen names (same thing for the Cancer society, which is also in this cluster). Notably, this cluster is not the only place where the "International Red Cross" shows up, suggesting multiple different nodes have this name, which reinforces the idea that the name is stolen. In the second, smaller cluster, we also find Amnesty International, which therefore can also be considered a victim of name theft. We can thus disclaim now Amnesty Internationnal and The Cancer Sociery that were suspected through our first analysis above.

GRAPH OFFSHORE

This graph is also very nice. We find again the "International Red Cross" and "Amnesty International" (and also "UNICEF"!), this time connected through a different intermediary. Clearly, there is more than one company out there ripping off the names of famous charities.

GRAPH BAHAMAS

While they lack the big charity names of the previous leaks, the bahamas papers nevertheless contain some information that is particularly relevant in Switzerland. Indeed, there is a cluster of three charities, United Way, Sandy and East Meets West, where the central nodes is...Credit Suisse, the second largest bank in Switzerland. A bit of research tells us that this is probably the real bank: Credit Suisse is listed as the third largest faciliator for offshore accounts in the Pana-ma Papers, and together with UBS is responsible for one in ten of all trusts leaked in the Baha-mas Papers. Credit Suisse. Our conclusion to this research is therefore the following: always do some research before do-nating to charities, and also before opening a bank account!
